[ Biology Laboratory ]

In this website, we aim to guide you through our performed laboratory experiment regarding the digestive system. Understanding our digestive system will allow us to gain a better idea on how other life forms digest their food. Understadnf the process of digestion may help us become more conscious of what we consume and what we put our bodies through. This simulation will help us understand several aspects of the process such as how the stomach moves and how food goes through the small intestine.

In doing our experiment, we first put bananas and crackers into a zip-locked bag, as well as water and orange juice. After that we crushed the contents of the bag. In doing this step, we figured out that the food that is being "digested" is the bananas and cracker while the water and orange juice represents the saliva and digestive juices as they both help in digestion as well as helping in crushing the food into a mushy substance. This means that the crushing of the food inside of the plastic bag represents and mimics the process of the stomach's digestion. Because of the mixture of the bananas and the crakcers we think that the food contains carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.

In the next process shown, the liquid flowing out of the stocking simulates the absorption of nutrients into the body. Correspondingly, the tray through which the liquid flows through represents the body. Inside the stocking, what remains is identified as partially digested waste. This residual material sets the stage for the step where the partially solidified contents are pushed, symbolizing the effective elimination of waste resulting from the digestive process. In essence, these stages, from nutrient absorption to waste elimination, offer a comprehensive understanding of the dynamic digestive system at play.

Among the paper towels tested, the one with four folds absorbed the largest amount of water. This observation suggests that the act of folding the paper towel allows more space for water absorption. Drawing a parallel to the human digestive system, the organ analogous to the paper towel is the small intestine. The small intestine's structure, characterized by numerous small folds known as villi, plays a crucial role in the absorption of nutrients. In this comparison, the efficiency of water absorption in the folded paper towel mirrors the effectiveness of nutrient absorption in the small intestine, emphasizing the significance of structural features in the absorption processes.

In the bile simulation experiment, we mixed liquid soap and oil, as well as oil and just water. We observed that mixing Oil and liquid soap caused liquid to emulsify, while the oil and water without soap in the other container did not combine or react. This happened because of a certain chemical both present in the liquid soap and bile which breaks down the fat in the oil/food that we eat. Thus, the bile created in our livers are a part of the chemical digestion.
